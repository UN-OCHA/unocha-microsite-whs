World Humanitarian Summit

23-24 May 2016, Istanbul, Turkey

Due to conflicts and disasters around the world, millions of people need humanitarian assistance in order to survive. As the scale of human suffering is now greater than at any time since the Second World War, former UN Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon convened the World Humanitarian Summit for the first time in the 70-year history of the United Nations. The aim was to generate commitments to reduce suffering and deliver better for people around the globe.

The Summit took place in Istanbul on 23 and 24 May 2016, bringing together 9,000 participants from 180 Member States, including 55 Heads of State and Government, hundreds of private sector representatives, and thousands of people from civil society and non-governmental organizations.

On A4H and PACT

The Agenda for Humanity and its five Core Responsibilities are part of the report “One Humanity, Shared Responsibility”, which guided the overarching framework of the World Humanitarian Summit. Participants at the Summit demonstrated overwhelming support for the Agenda for Humanity and announced thousands of commitments to turn it into reality. More than a dozen initiatives, partnerships, platforms and alliances were either newly developed or strengthened through the World Humanitarian Summit process and will help implement the Agenda for Humanity.

The Platform for Action, Commitments and Transformations (PACT) is a dynamic advocacy tool for all stakeholders involved in meeting humanitarian needs and reducing risk and vulnerability in their efforts to advance the Agenda for Humanity.

The PACT currently reflects all the commitments that were received in writing before or during the World Humanitarian Summit. Further commitments will be added to the platform to sustain the spirit of dynamic collaboration and ensure continued progress in advancing the Agenda for Humanity.

  • Political leadership to prevent and end conflicts
  • Uphold the norms that safeguard humanity
  • Leave no one behind
  • Change people's lives: from delivering aid to ending need
  • Invest in humanity

WHS Consultation Process

Between June 2014 and July 2015, an extensive and inclusive worldwide consultation process took place. More than 23,000 people in 153 countries were consulted, including humanitarian stakeholders and affected people and communities.

In February 2016, former United Nations Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon published his report “One Humanity, Shared Responsibility”. In the report, he called on Member States, the United Nations, humanitarian organizations and other relevant stakeholders to place humanity—people’s safety, dignity and their right to thrive—at the centre of global decision-making.

The Agenda for Humanity is a five-point plan that outlines the changes needed to alleviate suffering, reduce risk and lessen vulnerability on a global scale. It served as the overarching framework of the first-ever World Humanitarian Summit in Istanbul, held from 23 to 24 May 2016, asking global leaders to act on five Core Responsibilities.

Summit programme

The main Summit programme included:

  • World leaders came together at the High-Level Leaders’ Segment to reaffirm that common problems need common solutions and greater international cooperation. They underscored their responsibility to reduce and end conflicts through prevention and political solutions; provide greater investment in peaceful and inclusive societies; uphold and champion international humanitarian and human rights law; reach people who are furthest behind, including by reducing displacement and empowering and protecting women and girls; undertake a new way of working that not only meets people’s needs but reduces their risks and vulnerability; and acknowledge the need for sustained political and financial resources.

  • Seven high-level leaders’ round tables provided high-profile opportunities for Heads of State and Government and high-level representatives from other stakeholders to make commitments and announce bold actions in support of the five Core Responsibilities of the Agenda for Humanity. They made concrete policy, legal and operational commitments to advance them.

  • Fifteen special sessions responded to the specific calls made during the World Humanitarian Summit’s consultation process. They focused on launching strategic initiatives underlying the five Core Responsibilities of the Agenda for Humanity. These initiatives aim to harness the vision, skills and capacities of stakeholders in each relevant area. During the special sessions, Summit participants discussed current and emerging challenges, shared good practices and formed new partnerships.

  • At the Summit Announcement Plenary, Member States and other stakeholders announced ambitious and action-oriented commitments aimed at developing the priority action areas set out in the report “One Humanity, Shared Responsibility”.

  • Alongside the main programme, a number of side events were organized by different stakeholders in the form of briefings, seminars, workshops and panel discussions. The full programme is available below.

  • The World Humanitarian Summit Exhibition Fair showcased the work, products and programmes of Governments, organizations, agencies, companies and other institutions in support of humanitarian action.

  • The Innovation Marketplace showcased the practical applications of innovations, new or improved products, tools, services and processes that contribute to effective humanitarian action, with a special emphasis on small-scale innovations with high impact from underrepresented communities.

  • A series of special events took place during the World Humanitarian Summit. They included the launch of the Grand Bargain, the #Hack4Humanity, and other interactive and creative events.

FAQ

  • What was the WHS?

    The first ever World Humanitarian Summit, which took place in Istanbul on 23-24 May 2016, was a global call to action by former United Nations Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon. The Summit had three main goals:

    1. To re-inspire and reinvigorate a commitment to humanity and to the universality of humanitarian principles.
    2. To initiate a set of concrete actions and commitments aimed at enabling countries and communities to better prepare for and respond to crises, and be resilient to shocks.
    3. To share best practices which can help save lives around the world, put affected people at the center of humanitarian action, and alleviate suffering.
